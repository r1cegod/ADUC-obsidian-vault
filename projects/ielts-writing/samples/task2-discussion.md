> **TL;DR**: Band 8+ annotated discussion essay. Topic: parents vs schools in teaching social values.

## PROMPT

> Some people think parents are responsible for teaching children how to be good members of society. Others believe this is the role of schools. Discuss both views and give your own opinion.

---

## BRAINSTORM (before writing)

```
Body 1 (schools view — disagree with): Claim — schools provide structured, equitable delivery
                                        Mechanism — trained professionals + consistent environment
                                        Example — Finland curriculum with civic values embedded
Body 2 (parents view — agree with):    Claim — parents shape values in the pre-school years
                                        Mechanism — attachment + modelling precedes formal education
                                        Example — developmental psychology research on early moral formation
```

---

## RESPONSE

Whether the responsibility for socialising children falls primarily on parents or schools reveals deep assumptions about family and the role of education. This essay will examine both perspectives before arguing that while schools play an important supporting role, parents hold the greater responsibility.

Those who believe schools are best placed to teach social values argue that educational institutions offer a consistent, structured environment where trained professionals deliver these lessons equitably. Schools can reach children from all backgrounds and ensure that norms such as respect, fairness, and cooperation are communicated uniformly. Finland's national curriculum, which explicitly incorporates civic values alongside academic subjects, is often cited as evidence that schools can effectively shape students' social character. This view has clear merit, particularly for children whose home environments may not model positive behaviour.

Nevertheless, the stronger case supports parents as the primary agents of social development. Children begin forming values and social instincts in infancy, years before they enter formal education. The quality of early attachment, the boundaries parents set, and the values modelled within the household establish a foundation that schooling builds upon rather than creates from scratch. Research in developmental psychology consistently shows that children raised in households with clear moral frameworks display greater empathy and social competence by school age. Schools, however skilled, cannot substitute for this foundational influence.

In conclusion, while schools make a valuable contribution to children's social development, I believe parents bear the greater responsibility. The early years — which occur entirely within the family — are the most formative period for social learning. Schools reinforce what is established at home, but they cannot replace it.
