> **TL;DR**: Band 8+ annotated problem-solution essay. Topic: urban traffic congestion — causes and measures.

## PROMPT

> Many cities around the world are experiencing severe traffic congestion. What are the causes of this problem, and what measures could be taken to address it?

---

## BRAINSTORM (before writing)

```
Body 1 (causes): Claim — inadequate public transport creates car dependency
                 Mechanism — peripheral areas underserved → people buy cars → roads overwhelmed
                 Example — developing economies (Vietnam, Indonesia) with fast motorisation
Body 2 (solutions): Claim — invest in integrated public transport + congestion charging
                    Mechanism — supply-side (more buses/rail) + demand-side (price signal)
                    Example — Singapore MRT (supply) + London/Stockholm charging (demand)
```

---

## RESPONSE

Traffic congestion has become a defining challenge for urban centres globally, imposing significant economic and environmental costs on residents and businesses alike. The main causes of this problem are inadequate public transport infrastructure and unchecked growth in private vehicle ownership, and several policy measures can effectively address these issues.

The primary driver of urban congestion is the failure of public transport systems to keep pace with rapid urbanisation. As cities expand outwards, peripheral areas are frequently underserved by buses and trains, leaving private car ownership as the only practical option for many residents. This creates a self-reinforcing cycle: poor public transport increases car dependency, which in turn worsens congestion and discourages further investment in alternatives. In rapidly motorising economies such as Vietnam and Indonesia, where vehicle ownership rates are rising sharply but infrastructure expansion lags behind, this gap is particularly pronounced.

Addressing congestion effectively requires a two-pronged approach. Governments should invest substantially in integrated public transport networks, particularly rail and rapid bus transit serving underserved suburbs. Singapore's Mass Rapid Transit system demonstrates the potential: by connecting outer districts directly to employment centres, it has maintained consistently low congestion levels despite high population density. Simultaneously, demand-side measures such as congestion charging — levying fees on vehicles entering city centres during peak hours — have proven effective in cities including London and Stockholm, where traffic volumes fell by 20–30% while generating revenue for further transport investment.

In conclusion, urban traffic congestion stems primarily from inadequate public transport and unchecked car ownership. Expanding integrated transit while simultaneously disincentivising private vehicle use through pricing mechanisms offers the most durable solution. Cities that implement both strategies together are likely to see the greatest sustained reductions in congestion.

## BAND 8+ MOVES

```
Intro: previews causes + solutions     ← thesis must address BOTH parts; Band 5 only names causes
"self-reinforcing cycle"               ← captures the mechanism with a precise conceptual phrase
"particularly pronounced"              ← qualifies the example; shows awareness of context
"two-pronged approach"                 ← signals structured solution; examiner knows 2 body subs
"demonstrates the potential"           ← analytical framing; not just "for example Singapore"
Supply + demand both addressed         ← Band 8 move — structural + behavioural solution is complete
```
