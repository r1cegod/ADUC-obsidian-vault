> **TL;DR**: Band 8+ annotated adv-dis essay (advantages outweigh). Topic: gap year between school and university.

## PROMPT

> In many countries, young people are encouraged to take a gap year between finishing school and starting university. Do the advantages of this outweigh the disadvantages?

---

## BRAINSTORM (before writing)

```
Body 1 (disadvantages — weaker side): Claim — disrupts academic momentum + unequal access
                                       Mechanism — cost barrier favours wealthy; discipline loss
                                       Example — financial barrier for lower-income families
Body 2 (advantages — stronger side):  Claim — develops independence and clarifies direction
                                       Mechanism — self-directed experience → better university readiness
                                       Example — American Gap Association data: higher GPA + lower dropout
```

---

## RESPONSE

Gap years — periods of independent travel or work between secondary school and university — are increasingly common across many developed nations. While there are genuine drawbacks to this practice, I believe the advantages clearly outweigh the disadvantages.

The most frequently cited concern is that a gap year disrupts academic momentum. Students who spend twelve or more months away from formal study may find it difficult to readjust to the intellectual demands of university. There is also a financial dimension: gap years are expensive, and students from lower-income families may be forced into low-paid work rather than meaningful travel or volunteering, creating an unequal experience across socioeconomic groups. These limitations are real and should not be minimised.

However, the personal and academic benefits of a well-structured gap year are substantially greater. Taking time away from education allows young people to develop independence, self-awareness, and resilience — qualities that classroom learning alone rarely cultivates. Students who volunteer or work abroad return with enhanced problem-solving skills and, crucially, a clearer sense of academic purpose. Research by the American Gap Association found that gap year participants reported higher first-year university grade point averages and lower dropout rates than peers who enrolled immediately after school. This evidence suggests that the break, far from harming academic performance, may actively support it by helping students arrive with genuine motivation rather than passive momentum.

In conclusion, while gap years carry real risks — particularly academic disruption and unequal access — these concerns are outweighed by the documented benefits to personal development and academic engagement. The key is approaching the year with structure and purpose. Governments and universities that support structured gap year programmes are investing in better-prepared students.
