> **TL;DR**: Band 8+ annotated opinion essay (fully agree). Topic: government spending on rail vs roads.

## PROMPT

> Some people believe that governments should spend money on building fast rail networks rather than constructing new roads. To what extent do you agree or disagree?

---

## BRAINSTORM (before writing)

```
Body 1: Claim — rail reduces pollution / Mechanism — rail moves hundreds per journey vs cars
        Example — Japan Shinkansen, reduced domestic flights
Body 2: Claim — rail generates durable economic benefits / Mechanism — connects regions, no capacity ceiling
        Example — South Korea KTX, revitalised mid-sized cities
```

---

## RESPONSE

The debate over whether government transport investment should prioritise high-speed rail over road construction is increasingly relevant as cities face mounting congestion and environmental pressures. I firmly believe that investing in fast rail networks is the more prudent approach, primarily because it addresses both pollution and long-term economic growth more effectively than road expansion.

The most compelling argument for rail investment is its environmental advantage. Building new roads inevitably encourages greater private car use, which increases carbon emissions and worsens air quality. Rail networks, by contrast, can transport hundreds of passengers simultaneously while producing a fraction of the emissions per journey. Japan's Shinkansen network, for instance, carries over 420,000 passengers daily and has directly reduced domestic flight demand, lowering national carbon output. This demonstrates that high-speed rail is not merely a transport upgrade but an environmental necessity in the context of climate commitments.

Furthermore, fast rail generates more durable economic returns than road expansion. New roads reach capacity quickly and require continuous maintenance, creating a cycle of expenditure without lasting structural benefit. Rail infrastructure, however, stimulates development along its routes, connecting cities and reducing the economic isolation of peripheral regions. South Korea's KTX system, introduced in 2004, revitalised several mid-sized cities by cutting travel times from Seoul by over half, attracting investment and reducing population concentration in the capital. This illustrates how rail compounds in value over time, whereas road building offers diminishing returns.

In conclusion, I believe the evidence firmly supports prioritising fast rail over road construction. Rail addresses environmental sustainability and long-term regional development simultaneously, whereas road expansion provides only temporary relief to transport pressure. Governments that commit to rail infrastructure today are likely to reap significant dividends over the decades ahead.
