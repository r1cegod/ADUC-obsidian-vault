> **TL;DR**: Band 8+ annotated two-part question essay. Topic: rural-to-urban migration — why it happens, what problems it creates.

## PROMPT

> In many countries, people are moving away from rural areas and into cities. Why do people make this move? What problems does this create?

---

## BRAINSTORM (before writing)

```
Body 1 (why — causes): Claim — economic opportunity + access to services concentrated in cities
                        Mechanism — higher wages, wider job range, better education/healthcare
                        Example — rational choice for rural youth with limited local prospects
Body 2 (problems): Claim — urban overcrowding + rural community decline
                   Mechanism — housing demand rises → rents → informal settlements; rural ageing
                   Example — South/Southeast Asia, Africa, Latin America informal settlements
```

---

## RESPONSE

Urbanisation — the large-scale movement of people from rural to urban areas — is one of the most significant demographic trends of the twenty-first century. In my view, this migration is driven primarily by economic opportunity and unequal access to services, and it creates serious problems for both the cities receiving migrants and the communities they leave behind.

The most powerful driver of rural-to-urban migration is the concentration of economic opportunity in cities. Wages in urban centres are typically higher than in agricultural regions, and cities offer a far broader range of employment across industries including technology, finance, and manufacturing. For young people in rural areas with limited local job prospects, migration is often a rational economic decision. Beyond income, cities also provide access to higher-quality education, healthcare, and infrastructure that rural areas frequently lack, making urban life materially appealing even for those not primarily motivated by earnings.

However, the concentration of population in cities creates significant problems on both ends of the migration flow. Rapid influxes of migrants strain housing supply, driving up rents and generating overcrowded informal settlements in cities across South and Southeast Asia, Africa, and Latin America. Municipal governments struggle to extend water, sanitation, and transport services fast enough to match population growth, creating chronic infrastructure deficits. Meanwhile, the communities left behind face their own crisis: an ageing population, labour shortages in agriculture, and school and hospital closures as services become unviable at reduced population levels.

In conclusion, rural-to-urban migration is driven by rational pursuit of better economic and social conditions, a dynamic that is unlikely to change without deliberate policy intervention. Governments must invest in rural infrastructure and regional economic development to reduce the pressure that concentrated urbanisation places on cities and the communities people leave behind.
