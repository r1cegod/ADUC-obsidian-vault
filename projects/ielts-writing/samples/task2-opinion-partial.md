> **TL;DR**: Band 8+ annotated opinion essay (partial). Topic: whether zoos should be closed.

## PROMPT

> Some people think that zoos are cruel and should be closed permanently. To what extent do you agree or disagree?

---

## BRAINSTORM (before writing)

```
Body 1 (opposing — acknowledge): Claim — captivity restricts natural behaviour
                                  Mechanism — large animals need vast ranges, captivity stresses them
                                  Example — RSPCA research on large mammal stress indicators
Body 2 (own position — stronger): Claim — accredited zoos serve critical conservation role
                                   Mechanism — coordinated breeding + reintroduction programmes
                                   Example — Arabian oryx: extinct in wild 1972, reintroduced via zoo breeding
```

---

## RESPONSE

The question of whether zoos should be permanently closed is one that divides opinion sharply. While the argument that zoos restrict natural animal behaviour has genuine validity, I believe that modern, well-regulated zoos serve too important a conservation function to justify abolition.

It is true that confining animals to artificial environments raises legitimate welfare concerns. Animals such as elephants and great apes have natural ranges spanning hundreds of kilometres, and even the most spacious zoo enclosures offer only a fraction of this space. Research by the Royal Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals has documented elevated stress indicators in captive large mammals, suggesting that confinement imposes genuine psychological costs. These concerns deserve acknowledgement and cannot simply be dismissed as sentimental.

However, the conservation role of accredited zoos substantially outweighs these welfare drawbacks in many cases. Zoos now participate in coordinated breeding programmes for endangered species that would otherwise face extinction. The Arabian oryx, for example, was declared extinct in the wild in 1972, yet was successfully reintroduced to Oman and Jordan through zoo-based breeding programmes over the following decades. Without this intervention, the species would not exist today. Similarly, zoos fund field conservation research that benefits wild populations directly, making them active participants in biodiversity protection rather than mere exhibition spaces.

In conclusion, while concerns about animal welfare in captivity are well-founded, I do not believe they justify the wholesale closure of zoos. A more measured position — retaining accredited conservation zoos while closing those that fail to meet welfare standards — better serves both animal welfare and species preservation. The goal should be raising standards, not abolition.
