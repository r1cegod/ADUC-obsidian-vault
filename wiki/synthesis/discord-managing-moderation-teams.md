> **TL;DR**: Discord frames good moderation teams as structured human systems: moderators need shared standards, clear roles, specialization, and enough familiarity with each other to sustain trust and avoid chaos.

## Summary
This article is one of the strongest official sources on moderation governance. Discord argues that as communities grow, moderators should not remain a flat group of interchangeable generalists. Instead, teams should create structure, allocate responsibility, and let people specialize in areas such as chat moderation, bot management, and event support. The article also emphasizes knowing your team well enough to understand strengths, weaknesses, and when someone needs help. That makes it central for moving this domain beyond “server setup” into “staff operations.”

## Key Points
- Large teams need clear structure rather than one undifferentiated moderator role
- Specialization reduces the “jack of all trades, master of none” failure mode
- Team health and familiarity matter because moderation is collaborative and high-friction work

## Details
The strongest operational insight here is that moderation scale creates coordination problems before it creates pure permission problems. A large team benefits from sub-teams, delegated domains, and leaders who can coordinate the whole rather than leaving everyone on equal footing with unclear boundaries. The article also legitimizes technical-specialist roles such as bot managers, which matters for communities that rely heavily on custom moderation tooling.
